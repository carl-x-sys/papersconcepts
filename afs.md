# Agentic Federation System

*Thesis Paper*

**Agentic Federation System (AFS): An architectural evolution of the multi-agent system oriented toward projects whose scope spans multiple professional domains.**

---

**Preface**

The following paper presents a conceptual framework and architectural proposal. The ideas developed here — including the proposed Agentic Federation System (AFS), its constituent tiers, its storage layer, and its potential implications for the design of distributed AI architectures and the organisations that adopt them — are not presented as established facts or empirically validated findings. They constitute a structured theoretical proposition, intended to identify a problem space, articulate a possible approach, and define the questions that would need to be answered for that approach to be evaluated rigorously.

The proposal draws on observable limits of current multi-agent system architectures and on the structural properties of complex multi-domain work, but the conclusions derived from these observations are speculative in nature. Where claims are made about potential advantages or efficiencies, these should be understood as hypotheses motivating further investigation, not as demonstrated results. The challenges identified are likewise preliminary — they represent the most immediately visible open questions, not a complete risk assessment.

The AFS presupposes the availability of the Artificial Intelligence Language (AIL) as the inter-model communication protocol on which federated cooperation depends. Readers unfamiliar with AIL are referred to the companion paper for a treatment of the protocol, its translation layer, and its ontological function. A further companion paper develops the organisational model of accountable autonomy that corresponds to the AFS as a technical substrate; the present paper draws on that model without redeveloping it.

The purpose of this document is to open a line of inquiry, not to close one.

---

**The Need for an Evolution of Multi-Agent Systems**

Contemporary AI relies predominantly on large language models operating either in isolation or within multi-agent systems. Monolithic LLMs, while expressively broad, incur substantial costs in energy consumption, inference latency, and accuracy degradation as complexity increases. Multi-agent systems mitigate these costs by decomposing work across cooperating agents with defined roles, reducing the load on any individual model and enabling the use of smaller, more specialised models where appropriate.

Current architectures — process automation, algorithmic pipelines, and multi-agent systems — handle work whose complexity they can accommodate. Process automation handles predictable, structured, recurring work whose form is known in advance. Multi-agent systems extend this to work whose precise decomposition is discovered as the agents engage with it, provided that decomposition remains within a shared context and a common orchestration regime.

What these architectures do not cover is work whose complexity, when engaged with, exceeds what a shared context can accommodate. Such work reveals structure that spans epistemic boundaries: vocabularies, methods, and reasoning patterns that cannot be collapsed into a single coordinating frame without sacrificing the fidelity that makes each contribution worth having. A multi-agent system attempting to hold such work within its shared context either flattens the epistemic distinctions between contributing domains — sacrificing domain-specific precision for coordination feasibility — or multiplies direct agent-to-agent communication to a degree that erodes the efficiency gains the architecture was designed to produce. In either case, the architecture reintroduces the inefficiencies it was intended to resolve.

This is where the contribution of a new approach lies. What is needed is an architecture addressing the class of work that current architectures cannot accommodate: work whose complexity exceeds what any single shared context can hold, whose structure spans genuinely distinct epistemic domains, and whose effective pursuit requires coordination _between_ coherent collectives rather than _within_ one. The architectural shift required is specific: the locus of role assignment moves from the individual agent to the unit of competence, so that epistemically distinct domains are preserved as coherent units rather than dissolved into a single coordinating frame. This shift — from agent-level roles within a single collective to unit-level roles across a federation of collectives — is the architectural pivot on which the present proposal rests.

---

**The Parallel Structure of Complex Work**

Complex real-world work is rarely confined to a single domain of knowledge or practice. The development of a new vehicle battery draws simultaneously on electrochemistry, electrical engineering, mechanical construction, thermal management, safety regulation, and supply-chain economics. Each domain possesses its own body of knowledge, methodological conventions, and evaluative criteria, and none reduces meaningfully to another. Yet the work's initial expression may be a short prompt — _"design a low-cost battery with long lifetime and fast recharging"_ — whose brevity belies the complexity of the workstreams that engagement will reveal.

Work of this kind exhibits a structural property of architectural consequence: its domains are, for substantial portions of the solution process, _independently advanceable_. The electrochemist and the mechanical engineer need not proceed in lock-step; each makes progress within their own domain, synchronising with others only at points of genuine interdependence. A monolithic LLM, and to a lesser extent a single MAS, imposes an artificial serialisation on this inherently parallel structure. Distributing the work across multiple specialised systems — each a coherent collective within a domain — restores the work's natural parallelism and yields a qualitative improvement in the fidelity of reasoning within each domain.

The gap between input and structure is worth noting in its own right. A simple prompt can produce complex work, and a detailed brief can produce work that is extensive but structurally straightforward. The architectural question is not how the work enters but what its engagement reveals. Work whose engagement reveals structure that no single shared context can hold is the work for which the broader approach is needed.

**The Core Idea: The Agentic Federation System (AFS)**

The Agentic Federation System (AFS) is proposed as the architectural response. An AFS is a network of semi-autonomous multi-agent systems, each constituted as an expert for a defined domain, that cooperate across domain boundaries to serve shared objectives. Where a MAS coordinates agents within a single collective, an AFS coordinates experts across domains — a structural shift from team to federation.

Federation describes the core of the AFS. Each constituent expert retains internal sovereignty: its own agents, its own orchestration, its own domain-specific models and representations. Cooperation between experts occurs through defined interfaces rather than through centralised control. No single authority dictates the internal behaviour of any member; coordination is achieved through protocol rather than hierarchy. This preserves the epistemic integrity of each domain — electrochemistry is not forced to reason within mechanical engineering's methods, nor the reverse — while enabling structured interaction at the points where domains must interact.

Across the federation, all inter-model communication occurs through a common machine-native protocol. This role is served by the Artificial Intelligence Language (AIL), previously introduced as a compressed semantic notation and universal inter-agent communication protocol. AIL carries all communication between experts and between their coordinating roles; communication with human principals passes through the translation layer described in the AIL framework. Through this protocol, federated domains exchange intent, results, and dependencies without the losses incurred by natural-language intermediation.

The AFS concept rests on three commitments: domain-native specialisation within each expert, federated cooperation across expert boundaries, and a shared communication protocol that makes such cooperation tractable. Together, these yield an architecture whose capacity scales not by enlarging individual models but by extending the federation.

The framework is best understood across two dimensions: its foundational concept as a federation of experts coordinated through accountability, and its operational realisation as an architectural stack. These are described in turn.

**Part A — Foundational Concept of the AFS**

This section defines what the AFS is as a federation and what its constituent roles represent. These properties are independent of any specific architectural implementation and describe the nature and purpose of the AFS as a concept.

**A.1 The Federation of Experts**

The AFS is oriented toward _projects_: units of work whose complexity, when engaged with, reveals structure that no single shared context can hold. A project may enter the federation as a short prompt or as a detailed brief; what defines it is not the input but the engagement. A project's domains, its internal decomposition, and the interdependencies between its parts are discovered as the federation works on it, not prescribed as preconditions. Projects decompose into _workstreams_, each a sustained strand of activity within the project. A project consists of one or more workstreams, typically multiple and often interdependent, and each workstream is assigned to a single expert. Workstreams are what experts execute; projects are what the federation as a whole pursues.

At its foundation, an AFS is a _mesh of experts_. An expert is a bounded unit of competence: a multi-agent system whose coordinated agents collectively fulfil a defined capability. The capability is the expert's role within the federation; the agents within the expert have functions and aptitudes that together realise that capability. This is the architectural distinction on which the federation rests: roles in the AFS are assigned to experts, not to agents. An agent within an expert does not represent a role; it contributes a function to the role its expert carries.

Experts differ in the balance between knowledge and skill that defines their competence, and this balance is consequential for how the federation is composed. Some experts are primarily knowledge-oriented — their value lies in a body of domain understanding that they apply, interpret, and extend. Others are primarily skill-oriented — their value lies in a capacity for execution within a practice. Most real experts combine both in some proportion; the balance determines the kind of work the expert is suited to and the way its internal agents are organised.

The mesh is a _capability pool_, not a task-assignment hierarchy. Membership in the federation entails availability, not subordination. An expert does not belong to any particular consumer of its services; it exposes its capability to the federation and is drawn upon as projects require. A single expert may be engaged simultaneously by multiple concurrent projects, constrained only by its own orchestration and resource limits. The composition of the federation is itself dynamic: experts are instantiated as project load demands and retired when no longer required, so that the federation's steady-state footprint reduces to the experts that current projects actually require together with the coordinating roles described below.

Within the mesh, responsibility and accountability are distributed according to a deliberate pattern. Every expert carries _responsibility_ — the obligation to perform within its domain, to deliver within its capability, and to contribute to the projects it is engaged on. This responsibility is consistent with semi-autonomy: each expert retains latitude in how it fulfils it, which is what preserves the epistemic integrity of each domain. But a federation composed only of responsible experts with no accountability for the project as a whole would have no one to answer for whether a project succeeds, whether its composed output coheres, or whether the federation's collective action serves the organisation. Without defined accountability, the federation collapses into inordinateness at the level of outcomes even as its individual experts operate competently at the level of contributions.

The AFS therefore distinguishes _substantive experts_ — experts whose competence is a knowledge or skill domain such as electrochemistry, writing, or regulatory analysis — from _specialised experts_ whose competence is the coordinative and integrative work that accountability requires. Two specialised experts are foundational to the concept.

A _supervisor_ is the expert whose competence is the representation of an organisational unit's mandate and the orchestration of projects undertaken within that mandate. A unit's mandate, in the usage adopted here, comprises three components. First, the **services** the unit owes the organisation — the capabilities the unit is committed to providing, the basis on which the unit's existence within the organisation is justified. Services are unique within the organisation: two units do not provide the same service, and unit boundaries are maintained accordingly. Second, the **boundaries** of the unit — the scope within which the unit acts, which it maintains against neighbouring units and which the federation respects in routing projects. Third, the unit's **internal strategy** — how the unit develops and enhances its services, and how it positions its capabilities for the organisation's evolving needs. Together these three components constitute what the supervisor represents on behalf of its unit; _mandate_ serves as the umbrella term where compactness is needed.

A supervisor draws on whichever substantive experts its projects require; a supervisor may use many experts, and an expert may be used by many supervisors. The supervisor is accountable for the conduct of the projects it owns: that they are undertaken, that they are pursued within the unit's mandate, that they serve the organisation's strategy, and that their outcomes are deliverable. A supervisor's representation of mandate is not autonomous advocacy but advocacy within the organisation's strategic frame, which serves as both guideline — the direction toward which inter-mandate reconciliation should tend — and guardrail — the boundary beyond which no reconciled outcome is acceptable.

A _merger_ is the expert whose competence is the reconciliation and verification of project content. Because each substantive expert optimises within its domain, the composed output of a multi-domain project is not guaranteed to form a coherent whole: a battery design optimised purely for electrochemical performance may be mechanically infeasible; a strategic recommendation optimised for one market condition may be inconsistent with the technical constraints another expert has established. The merger receives from the supervisor, at project initiation, a brief containing the project's objectives, the conditions under which it must be pursued — frame conditions, applicable strategy, and any constraints arising from prior mandate reconciliation with peer supervisors — and the substantive experts identified as required for the work. From that point the merger activates those experts, examines the contributions assembled in a project's shared workspace, identifies inconsistencies between them, verifies the composed result against the project's objectives, and either returns work to contributing experts for revision or releases the composed output as the project's verified result. The merger may add experts to the set during the project where the work reveals additional needs; the supervisor is informed and may object on mandate or strategic grounds. Questions of mandate or strategy that arise mid-project are not resolved by the merger but surfaced to the supervisor, whose accountability they remain. The merger is accountable for the coherence and verification of project content; it is the project-level analogue of the reviewer agent that operates within each substantive expert.

The distinction between responsibility and accountability gives the federation its structural integrity without compromising its semi-autonomous character. Substantive experts remain free in how they pursue their work; supervisors and mergers carry the accountability that ensures the federation's collective action is coherent, attributable, and aligned with the organisation it serves.

**A.2 The AFS as an Interaction Framework**

The interaction between experts, supervisors, and mergers constitutes the operational life of the federation. Five patterns are foundational.

_Project intake and decomposition._ A project enters the federation through the supervisor whose unit's mandate it most directly concerns. That supervisor creates the project's storage and populates it with the project as initially expressed. It then determines the project's frame conditions and applicable strategy, conducts any cross-supervisor notification and mandate reconciliation the project requires, and identifies the substantive experts whose capabilities the work appears to need, drawing on a representation of the federation's capability landscape and on the organisation's strategy. The product of this front-loaded work is a brief that is handed to a merger: the project's objectives, the conditions under which it must be pursued, and the experts identified as required. From this handoff the project transitions into execution under the merger; the supervisor remains accountable for the project's conduct and for any mandate or strategic question that may arise during execution. The decomposition the brief encodes is context-preserving: each workstream the experts will execute carries the relational information needed for its result to be composed with the results of its siblings.

_Cross-supervisor notification._ Because the mandates supervisors represent are not mutually exclusive, most non-trivial projects bear on more than one unit. When a supervisor originates a project whose execution will touch another unit's mandate — its services, its boundaries, or its internal strategy — the affected supervisors are notified and given the opportunity to assert their concerns. Notification is conditional: supervisors are informed only where their mandate is materially touched, to avoid saturating the federation with irrelevant traffic. Material relevance is determined through the structured representation of unit mandates maintained by the federation, complementing the representation of expert capabilities that supports the decomposition pattern.

_Mandate reconciliation._ Where a project bears on the mandates of multiple supervisors, those supervisors negotiate under the frame conditions held in federation governance and toward the organisation's strategic objectives. Frame conditions are procedural: they define how negotiation occurs, what may be contested, and how unresolved positions are escalated. Strategy is substantive: it defines what the organisation is trying to achieve and what trade-offs it prefers when trade-offs are unavoidable. Together, frame conditions and strategy orient mandate reconciliation toward outcomes that serve the organisation as a whole rather than toward outcomes that merely balance the mandates of the units involved. No supervisor overrides another directly; the negotiation produces constraints within which the project's content is then pursued.

_Content reconciliation._ The merger activates the substantive experts identified in the project brief, providing each with its workstream and the project context drawn from project storage. As contributions accumulate in the shared workspace, the merger examines those contributions for coherence with one another and verifies the composed content against the project's objectives. Incompatibilities are returned to the relevant experts for revision; the verified composed result is released to the originating supervisor for delivery. The merger is engaged throughout the lifetime of a project, not only at its conclusion, so that incoherence is detected at the points where contributions from different experts first come into contact rather than at the point where rework is most expensive. Project storage also serves as a substrate for cross-domain attention: an expert reading a peer's contribution may identify something that bears on its own work — a constraint it must respect, an inconsistency with what it has produced, a question whose answer affects its approach. When this occurs, the expert surfaces the matter to the merger through the orchestrator-mediated channel rather than addressing the peer directly. The merger then determines the appropriate response: a clarification request, a revision instruction, a structured exchange between the experts involved, or — where the matter touches mandate or strategy — escalation back to the supervisor.

_Escalation to human judgement._ The federation is designed to handle complex work with reduced human involvement, not without it. Two patterns of human engagement are architectural commitments rather than exceptions. First, where mandate reconciliation between supervisors cannot be resolved within the frame conditions and strategy available, or where content reconciliation by the merger encounters a decision the available references cannot settle, the decision is escalated to a human principal. The federation does not force an automated resolution in cases where the references genuinely do not determine the outcome; it surfaces the decision and resumes on the basis of the human's judgement. Second, the composed output of a project is, before release, subject to human quality review. Project storage provides the record against which such review proceeds; the human confirms the composed result rather than reconstructing the work that produced it. The federation's goal is to reduce human interaction on work whose complexity the federation can handle itself, while preserving human authority over genuine decisions and over final approval of what the federation delivers.

The AFS thus operates under what is termed the _single-optimisation-to-overall-fit_ principle, which is realised through two complementary mechanisms. Mandate reconciliation, occurring in the supervisor layer under frame conditions and organisational strategy, establishes the bounds within which a project's content is pursued. Content reconciliation, occurring through the merger, ensures that the pursued content holds together as a coherent whole and serves the project's objectives. Where either mechanism encounters a decision it cannot settle on the references available, human escalation provides the resolution. Ontological alignment at the interfaces between experts is handled by the shared communication protocol, which encodes not only concepts but the relational dependencies between them.

**A.3 The AFS as the Technical Counterpart to an Accountability-Based Organisation**

The features described above — bounded expert capabilities, mandate representatives with dual technical and political function, content reconciliation as a distinct tier of accountability, negotiation under frame conditions and toward strategy rather than under hierarchical command — invite comparison with organisational structure. The comparison is apt, but not with organisations as most of them currently exist. Conventional organisations are characterised by hierarchical authority, constrained autonomy, and the subordination of unit mandates to directives descending from above. An AFS mirroring such a structure would forfeit the properties that make federation valuable in the first place.

The accurate isomorphism is with an organisational model grounded in _accountable autonomy_: organisational units that act independently within their domain of competence, that carry responsibility for the work they perform and accountability for the outcomes they own, and that cooperate through defined interfaces rather than through centralised command. In such a model, responsibility is distributed across all units — everyone is responsible for their work — while accountability is concentrated in specific roles whose competence is the orchestration and integration of the organisation's collective action. The AFS is the technical counterpart to an organisation of this kind. Its substantive experts carry responsibility; its supervisors and mergers carry accountability. Its coordination proceeds by protocol rather than by hierarchy, and toward organisational strategy rather than toward the aggregation of unit preferences.

**Part B — Architectural Layers of the AFS**

This section describes how the AFS is operationalised within a system. The layers below define the storage substrate on which the federation depends and the internal composition of the three active tiers — substantive experts, supervisors, and mergers — through which the federation performs its work.

**B.1 The Storage Layer**

The active tiers of the federation — substantive experts, supervisors, and mergers — are supported by a passive _storage layer_ that persists the information on which they depend. Four storage types are maintained, each serving a distinct function.

_Project storage_ is instantiated per project and holds the evolving state of that project throughout its lifecycle: the project brief produced by the originating supervisor, intermediate results from each expert engaged, shared context, decisions made, and open questions. It is created by the supervisor at project initiation and populated initially with the brief that the supervisor hands to the merger; from that point it is the shared workspace through which substantive experts working on the project can view current status and peer contributions, through which the merger examines accumulating content for coherence, and through which both the supervisor and the human quality reviewer follow progress. Project storage also serves as the substrate through which experts identify cross-domain matters that bear on their own work and surface them, through the merger, for resolution. Project storage is archived at project completion, where it remains available for later reference.

_Expert storage_ is per-expert and holds knowledge that supplements the trained model: domain information that was not included in training, information that has emerged since the last training cycle, and specialised references the expert draws upon during execution. Represented as a graph or vector structure, it permits the expert to remain current without continuous retraining; new information enters storage rather than requiring a new model generation. Secondarily, expert storage can serve as a curated source for periodic retraining, allowing information that has proven consistently useful to be absorbed into the model itself.

_Governance storage_ is federation-wide and holds four kinds of federation-level content. First, _rules and protocols_ that govern collective behaviour — how experts communicate, what constitutes an acceptable project outcome, how conflicts are to be negotiated, and the criteria for admitting, removing, or updating federation members. Second, _frame conditions_ — the procedural constraints within which mandate reconciliation between supervisors occurs, defining how negotiation proceeds, what may be contested, and how unresolved positions are escalated. Third, _organisational strategy_ — the substantive reference against which mandate reconciliation is oriented, articulating what the organisation is trying to achieve and what trade-offs it prefers when trade-offs are unavoidable. Fourth, two structured registries: the _capability registry_, representing which substantive experts exist and what they are expert at, consulted by supervisors during project decomposition; and the _mandate registry_, representing the services, boundaries, and internal strategies each supervisor has declared on behalf of its unit, consulted when determining which peer supervisors must be notified of a given project. Both registries are maintained centrally rather than per-supervisor, to avoid the synchronisation problems that per-supervisor views would introduce.

Governance storage is subject to a deliberate design posture: it should contain as much as is necessary to prevent disorder, and no more. Over-regulation constrains the creative latitude that makes federated expertise valuable; under-regulation permits the federation to fragment into incompatible behaviour. The calibration is itself a governance concern and is addressed among the open challenges.

_Supervisor storage_ is per-supervisor and holds the supervisor's representation of its unit's mandate: the services the unit owes the organisation, the boundaries the unit maintains against other units, and the internal strategy by which the unit develops its capabilities. It is the computational counterpart of a unit charter and is what makes a particular supervisor a particular supervisor rather than a generic orchestrator. The mandates held in supervisor storage are the source from which the federation's mandate registry is composed; the registry aggregates the declarations made in each supervisor's storage for federation-wide consultation.

**B.2 The Internal Structure of a Substantive Expert**

A substantive expert's internal agents are organised around four foundational functions.

_The Orchestrator_ coordinates the expert's internal work, reads governance storage to ensure the expert's operation conforms to federation rules, and serves as the expert's single point of contact with the rest of the federation. All communication between the expert and other parties in the federation is orchestrator-mediated.

_The Analyst_ understands the workstream and prepares it for solution, retrieving relevant information from expert storage and from the project's shared workspace, planning the approach, and producing a structured problem representation with an initial direction for the solution. For complex professions whose internal structure admits sub-domains — physics into mechanics, optics, and electronics; medicine into cardiology, neurology, and oncology — an expert may instantiate multiple analysts, each specialised to a sub-domain. An equivalent federation may instead instantiate multiple narrower experts that cooperate directly; the AFS is flexible on this point.

_The Designer_ produces the expert's deliverable: the solution, concept, or artefact for which the expert is responsible. Where multiple analysts have contributed, the designer integrates their work into a coherent brief before acting; it may return to an analyst for additional detail or clarification.

_The Reviewer_ performs quality assurance within the expert, checking the analyst's plan, the designer's concept, and the expert's final output for quality, completeness, and gaps. The reviewer may reject work and return it to the designer for revision; unresolved disagreements escalate to the orchestrator. The reviewer's role within the expert corresponds to the merger's role within the federation: internal review ensures the coherence of a single expert's output, while the merger ensures the coherence of the composed output across experts.

**B.3 The Internal Structure of a Supervisor**

A supervisor's internal agents are organised around three foundational functions.

_The Orchestrator_ coordinates the supervisor's internal work and serves as its point of contact with the rest of the federation — with originating parties outside the federation, with peer supervisors, with the mergers engaged on the supervisor's projects, and with the orchestrators of the substantive experts it has assigned workstreams to. All communication between the supervisor and other parties is orchestrator-mediated.

_The Governance Analyst_ interprets the contents of governance storage and applies them to the supervisor's operation. Its consultation extends beyond rules and protocols to include frame conditions and organisational strategy: when the supervisor originates a project, the governance analyst determines the frame conditions that apply and the strategic objectives toward which the project's conduct should tend; when the supervisor is notified of a peer's project that touches its mandate, it establishes the terms within which the supervisor's concerns may be asserted and the strategic considerations that bear on their assertion.

_The Dependence Mapper_ maintains the supervisor's view of how the federation's capabilities and its peers' mandates relate to the projects the supervisor owns. It consults the capability registry when decomposing a project and the mandate registry when originating one, identifying respectively the substantive experts whose capabilities are required and the peer supervisors whose mandates must be notified.

**B.4 The Internal Structure of a Merger**

A merger's internal agents are organised around four foundational functions.

_The Orchestrator_ coordinates the merger's internal work and serves as its point of contact with the supervisor whose project it is engaged on and with the orchestrators of the substantive experts contributing to that project. It is the channel through which the merger requests revisions and through which it releases verified composed results.

_The Coherence Analyst_ examines the contributions accumulating in project storage for internal consistency — whether the contributions of different substantive experts are compatible with one another. It is engaged continuously throughout the project's lifetime, identifying incompatibilities at the points where contributions from different experts first come into contact.

_The Verification Agent_ examines the composed content against the project's stated objectives. Its concern is distinct from coherence: a set of contributions may be internally consistent yet fail to meet the project's objectives, or may meet the objectives individually while failing to cohere as a composed whole. The verification agent asks whether the composed content does what the project requires it to do, independent of whether its parts agree with one another.

_The Reconciliation Agent_ formulates the revisions that coherence and verification findings require, determining which contributing experts must revise their work, what the revision must address, and what the revised contributions must satisfy. When coherence and verification are both satisfied, it prepares the composed result for release to the supervisor.

The agent counts described across B.2, B.3, and B.4 are baselines. Substantive experts, supervisors, and mergers whose work requires it may instantiate additional agents — multiple analysts specialised to sub-domains, a dedicated negotiation agent for sustained cross-supervisor reconciliation, multiple coherence analysts specialised to different cross-domain interfaces — without departing from the architectural pattern.

**Potential Advantages**

- **Parallel processing of complex work.** The federation's topology permits workstreams whose domains are independently advanceable to proceed in parallel rather than in sequence. Parallelism is inherent to the architecture, not an optimisation applied on top of it. The merger's continuous reconciliation further supports parallel execution by surfacing cross-domain incompatibilities systematically as contributions accumulate.
- **Efficiency through specialisation.** Each substantive expert is a small, narrowly trained model rather than a general-purpose large model. Energy cost, inference latency, and domain accuracy all benefit. The aggregate computational cost of a federated project is typically lower than processing the same project through a single large model, despite coordination overhead.
- **Maintainability without full retraining.** Expert storage permits knowledge updates without regenerating the underlying model. New information enters storage and is available at inference time; periodic retraining remains possible but is no longer a precondition for the expert to remain current.
- **Composable and extensible federation.** New substantive experts can be admitted without restructuring existing members. The capability registry is updated, protocols are inherited from governance storage, and the new expert becomes available to supervisors. Scaling is additive rather than transformative.
- **Fidelity of domain reasoning.** Each expert reasons within its own methods and vocabulary rather than within a representation generic enough to accommodate all domains within a single model. The cost of cross-domain reconciliation is paid at the interface, not within each model.
- **Structural separation of mandate from content.** Mandate reconciliation and content reconciliation are handled by different tiers, by different mechanisms, and against different criteria. Technical content is not decided by political negotiation, and political negotiation is not constrained by technical coherence considerations the merger will resolve independently.
- **Accountability and traceability.** Responsibility for work and accountability for outcomes are structurally distinguished. Substantive experts carry responsibility for what they contribute; supervisors and mergers carry accountability for the projects they own and the composed content they verify. Project storage persists the full record, so that when an outcome must be audited, the federation can reconstruct how it was reached and which accountable party was responsible at each stage.
- **Preservation of human authority.** The federation reduces human involvement on work whose complexity it can handle itself, while preserving human authority over decisions the federation's references cannot settle and over final approval of what the federation delivers. The reduction is an efficiency gain; the preservation is an architectural commitment.
- **Alignment with organisational reality.** Where the AFS is deployed as the technical substrate for an organisation built on accountable autonomy, architecture and organisation share a common structural logic. The cost of integration is materially lower than for architectures whose assumptions differ from the organisation's own.

**Challenges to Address**

- **Self-controlled mandate reconciliation.** Supervisors negotiate under frame conditions and toward organisational strategy, without an external arbiter below the level of human escalation. Frame conditions too specific become brittle; frame conditions too permissive fail to constrain conflict. Whether the federation can self-govern the evolution of its own frame conditions and its interpretation of strategy — and how infrequently escalation to human judgement must occur for the federation to deliver on its efficiency promise — is open.
- **Project decomposition.** Mapping an incoming project to the right subset of expert capabilities at the right granularity is an inference problem spanning the project's subject matter and the federation's capability landscape. Decomposition is not a single act but a distributed judgement: the supervisor identifies the experts initially required as part of the project brief, and the merger may extend the set during execution where the work reveals additional needs. Too coarse a decomposition under-exploits specialisation; too fine a decomposition imposes coordination overhead that erodes the federation's efficiency advantage. The challenge applies at both stages and to the boundary between them.
- **Material relevance determination.** Universal notification of peer supervisors saturates the federation; minimal notification risks decisions that prejudice units whose mandates should have been consulted. The mandate registry provides the structural substrate for the inference, but the inference itself — whether a given project's subject matter materially touches a given declared mandate — remains open.
- **Governance calibration.** Governance storage must contain enough to prevent fragmentation and not so much as to constrain the creativity that makes federated expertise valuable. The correct calibration depends on the federation's composition, its project mix, and its organisational context; the federation must have mechanisms to detect when governance is failing in either direction.
- **Ontology construction and maintenance.** Cross-domain communication depends on relational representations — encoded in AIL — that are shared across experts and kept consistent. Their construction, alignment at domain interfaces, and maintenance as domains evolve are challenges arising in both AIL and AFS contexts.
- **Sub-project merger structure.** A single merger is sufficient for projects whose complexity allows the supervisor to assign one merger to oversee content reconciliation across all contributing experts. For projects of substantial scope and cross-domain depth, however, this approach reaches its limit: the breadth of contributions and the variety of cross-domain interfaces exceed what one merger can hold. The architectural response is to decompose such projects into sub-projects, each with its own merger accountable for the sub-project's content reconciliation, while a further merger is engaged at the parent-project level to reconcile the sub-projects' composed outputs against one another and against the parent project's objectives. This is not a separate kind of merger; the merger at the parent level performs the same content-reconciliation work as any other merger, only over a broader scope of contributions — namely, the composed outputs of sub-project mergers rather than the outputs of substantive experts directly. The hierarchy that emerges is one of accountability scope, not of command: the parent-level merger does not direct the sub-project mergers' work; it reconciles the results of that work into a coherent whole at the parent level. The challenge is to determine, for any given project, when sub-project decomposition is warranted and how parent-level reconciliation is structured; both are open questions whose answer depends on the project's specific shape.
- **Calibration of human escalation.** The federation's design reserves human involvement for genuine decisions and final quality review. Calibrating the boundary is itself a challenge. A federation that escalates too readily fails to deliver on its efficiency promise; one that escalates too reluctantly exposes humans to final outputs that passed through automated verification despite containing decisions humans should have made. The merger's verification agent and the supervisors' governance analysts are the points at which escalation decisions arise, and the quality of their judgement about what warrants escalation is itself a federation-level property that must mature over time.
