# Federated Accountability Organisation

*Concept Paper*

**Federated Accountability Organisation (FAO): An evolutionary organisation model that replaces the intermediating layer of conventional hierarchy with a federation of accountable units.**

---

## Preface

This paper presents a model concept for organisational design. Like other organisational models, FAO is evaluated by coherence and fit rather than by empirical proof; its claims are propositions about how an organisation may be structured, not findings about how organisations behave. The purpose of the paper is to articulate the concept clearly enough that it can be examined, adopted in part, adapted, or contested.

FAO is positioned as an evolution of hierarchical organisation. It retains hierarchy where hierarchy structures responsibility and decision at the strategic level, and removes the intermediating layers whose function has become information passthrough rather than genuine management. It is distinct from models that dissolve hierarchy entirely; it does not replace authority with circles, constitutions, or facilitated processes. FAO's claim is that hierarchy serves a purpose where it structures, and that the cost of hierarchy lies in the layers added beyond what structure requires.

FAO is the organisational counterpart to a separate proposal — the Agentic Federation System (AFS) — which describes a technical architecture for AI cooperation under accountability. The two are designed as a co-evolved pair: FAO addresses the human-organisational dimension, AFS the technical-architectural dimension, of an underlying shift toward distributed accountability. This paper stands alone as an organisational argument. It treats AFS as the recommended technical substrate at scale; it does not depend on AFS for its argument.

The paper is structured as a set of principles. Each principle states what the FAO commits to, why the commitment matters, and how it operates. The principles are grouped into thematic sections that correspond to the foundational dimensions of the model: the federation of units, the portfolio, accountability and self-management, steering, the strategic level, and the career model. The principles within a section are ordered for readable comprehension; readers familiar with the model may consult them in any order.

## The Need for an Evolution of Hierarchical Organisation

Hierarchical organisation has earned its persistence. It provides clear lines of responsibility, defined paths for decision and escalation, and a structure within which accountability can be located. These are not incidental properties; they are what allow large organisations to coordinate work that no individual or small group can hold. The architectural critique of hierarchy is not that it organises, but that it has, in most contemporary organisations, accumulated layers beyond what organisation requires.

The accumulated layers are middle management whose function is no longer to manage but to intermediate: to route information upward, to route approvals downward, and to compress the signal between the people who understand the work and the people authorised to decide on it. Each such layer adds latency to decisions, distorts the information that strategic levels receive, and removes the locus of decision from the people best positioned to make it. The cost is not paid in salaries alone; it is paid in the organisation's capacity to act on what it knows.

Models that respond to this cost by dissolving hierarchy entirely address the cost of intermediation, but they sacrifice the structural clarity that hierarchy provides where it is genuinely useful. The strategic level still requires accountability; cross-unit conflicts still require resolution; the organisation as a whole still requires direction. Models that distribute these functions evenly across the organisation either fail to provide them or reconstitute them under different names.

What is needed is an evolution that preserves hierarchy where it structures and removes it where it merely intermediates. The architectural pivot is specific: the locus of accountability for *how* work is performed moves from the management chain to the unit that performs the work, while accountability for *whether* the organisation as a whole succeeds is retained at the strategic level. Responsibility is distributed across the people who do the work; accountability is concentrated where it must be visible — at the unit lead and at the strategic level — without intermediating layers between them.

The properties this evolution is intended to deliver are concrete:

- **Increased creativity at the level of the work**, because decisions are made by those who hold the relevant expertise.
- **Reduced information damage**, because signal does not pass through layers that compress and distort it.
- **Faster decisions**, because authority sits with the people who have the information.
- **Faster organisational adaptation**, because the structure itself is maintained as a living object rather than fossilised in an inherited chart.

## Part A — Foundational Concept of the FAO

This section defines what the FAO is as a federation and what its constituent elements represent. These properties are independent of any specific implementation and describe the nature and purpose of the FAO as a model concept. The federation is structurally a flat hierarchy: it preserves hierarchy at the strategic level and at the unit-lead level, and removes the intermediating layers between them by design. The principles below state what this commitment entails — for the federation's composition, for its portfolio, for accountability and self-management, for the rails by which the federation is steered, for the strategic level itself, and for the careers of the people whose work the federation depends on.

The principles are presented in three movements: the principle stated cleanly, the goal it serves, and the mechanism by which it operates. The principles within each section are ordered for readable comprehension.

### A.1 The Federation of Units

The organisation is a federation of *units*. A unit is a coherent group organised around a shared professional capability and committed to providing one or more services to the organisation. Each person in the organisation belongs to exactly one unit. Each unit has a unit lead — a single point of representation accountable for the unit's mandate. The principles below describe how units relate to the federation's conceptual structure, what their services are and how they are exchanged, and how the federation grows.

#### Two structures that operate together

The federation has *two structures that operate together*. The conceptual structure is built from domains, services, and capabilities — domains and services are unique within the organisation and may be subdivided; capabilities may exist in several units. The operational structure is the federation's hierarchy of accountability: the CEO unit, the strategic management units below it, and the unit leads below those. The conceptual structure maps onto the operational structure without being constrained to a one-to-one correspondence.

The two structures answer different questions. The conceptual structure organises what the organisation does and knows; the operational structure organises who is accountable for what. Conflating them — as conventional org charts do — distorts both. Separating them allows each to be calibrated independently: the domain map evolves with the organisation's substantive concerns, while the operational structure remains flat.

A unit holds one or more services within one or more sub-domains. In a small organisation, a single unit may cover several sub-domains; as the organisation grows, sub-domains separate into units of their own. The mapping is calibrated to the organisation's size and the complexity of its work, not fixed by a structural rule.

#### Three kinds of units

The federation comprises *three kinds of units*, distinguished by the kind of service they hold. Strategic management units hold services concerned with the organisation's overall direction. Business management units hold services concerned with the management of a defined area of organisational activity. Business operation units hold services concerned with the substantive work the organisation performs.

The three kinds are categories of service, not levels of authority. They give the federation a vocabulary for what each unit contributes without implying a chain of command. All three kinds operate through the same structural mechanisms — supply and demand, the steering rails, accountability — and the relationships between them are service relationships, not directive relationships.

A strategic management unit demands and consumes services from the other kinds in the same way any unit does. Steering from a strategic management unit reaches business operation units when those units demand the service; it is not pushed down a chain. The kinds describe the federation's composition; they do not describe its authority structure.

#### Service as commitment

A *service* is a sustained, available offering of value to one or more consumers — internal units, external customers, or both. A service may take the form of a product, a deliverable, an advisory output, a decision, a process artefact, a piece of software, or any other output of a unit that is consumed by others.

The defining property of a service is the commitment to make it available, not the form of its output. Treating products and services as separate categories — as conventional vocabulary does — obscures what they share: a unit's sustained obligation to provide something that other parties consume. The FAO unifies the category so that the same structural rules apply to every commitment a unit holds.

A unit declares its services in the portfolio. The declaration is what other units consult when they seek the service; the unit's existence is justified by the services it commits to provide.

#### Service uniqueness and capability bundling

*Services are unique within the organisation*; the same service is not offered twice. Capabilities, by contrast, are not unique — the same capability may exist in several units. When two units would otherwise hold capability sets that together form the same service, the capability is bundled into a single unit and offered as a service from there.

Uniqueness gives the federation its economic discipline. Where the same service exists in multiple units, the federation pays for parallel work, accumulates inconsistencies between the parallel versions, and obscures the question of which unit is accountable for the service's outcome. Bundling preserves functional depth — the consolidated unit develops genuine expertise — and makes accountability locatable.

When a unit needs a capability it does not itself hold, it consumes the service of the unit that does. When two units find themselves developing similar capability sets to similar ends, the capability is consolidated into one unit and offered as a service. Capability remains where it is naturally distributed; the service rule operates on what is committed and consumed.

#### Service composition

When a service grows beyond what one unit can hold while remaining coherent, or when regulatory or structural independence requires it, the service is *decomposed into constituent services* held by multiple units. The composed service has an accountable owner — typically a unit lead within the same domain — who is accountable for the composed whole; the constituent services remain with their respective units, each accountable for its part.

Some services are too large or too multi-faceted to be held by a single unit, but they remain coherent as a single offering. Forcing such a service into one unit produces an oversized unit that the lead cannot hold; distributing the service without a defined owner produces a service no one is accountable for. Composition gives the federation a way to hold large services without enlarging units beyond their natural limits.

The composed service's accountable owner integrates the work of the contributing units, manages the relationships between them, and represents the composed service to its consumers. The contributing units operate autonomously on their constituent services within the agreed integration.

#### Mandate

Every unit holds a *mandate*. The mandate has three components: the services the unit commits to provide, the boundaries it maintains against neighbouring units, and the internal strategy by which it operates within the organisation's strategy. The mandate is what the unit is for.

The mandate gives the unit a defined existence within the federation. Without an explicit mandate, a unit's purpose is ambiguous, its boundaries are contested, and its relationships to other units are unstable. The mandate makes the unit's role articulable, so that the unit can be held accountable for what it has committed to and so that other units know what they may expect of it.

The mandate is declared in the portfolio and is the basis on which the unit is admitted to the federation. It evolves with the unit through deliberate revision rather than drift; revisions affect the unit's relationships with consumers and peers and are visible to the federation through the portfolio.

#### Unit-level governance

Every unit has its own *internal governance*, extending the organisation's governance, that says how the unit operates. The unit's governance must extend the organisation's, never conflict with it.

Governance at the unit level lets the unit calibrate its own operation to its profession and the services it holds, while remaining bound by the federation's general rules. Without unit-level governance, the federation would impose a uniform operating model on units whose work is genuinely different; with unit-level governance, each unit operates appropriately to its work while the federation as a whole remains coherent.

The unit's governance is set by the lead in accordance with the federation's governance. It covers internal decision-making, internal review, the unit's interpretation of organisation-wide protocols, and any further rules the unit needs to operate. It is part of what the unit declares to the federation through the portfolio.

#### Supply and demand

Services are exchanged through *supply and demand*. A unit consumes services it needs; a unit provides services it has committed to. Demand drives consumption; commitment drives provision. Services are not pushed by providers onto consumers, and consumers do not impose obligations on providers beyond the commitment the provider has made.

Supply and demand makes the federation's internal exchange economically real. Services that no one demands are not consumed and become candidates for retirement; services that meet demand sustain themselves through the consumption they attract. The mechanism keeps the federation honest about which of its commitments produce value and which do not.

A unit needing a service queries the portfolio for the unit that provides it, and consumes the service on the terms the providing unit has declared. Consumption may be metered, costed, and accounted for through whatever economic mechanism the organisation adopts; the FAO does not prescribe a specific mechanism but does prescribe that consumption is visible and that the relationship is structured by the consumer's demand.

#### Drivers shape services

Services and their delivery are shaped by the *drivers of the contexts in which they operate*. External services are shaped by commercial dynamics — customer demand, competitive pressure, market conditions. Internal services are shaped by the demand of consuming units. Both are also shaped by external regulation, technology shifts, and other environmental factors. The federation accommodates multiple driver patterns simultaneously.

No single driver explains all the federation's services. Treating commercial dynamics as the only relevant driver — as customer-centric models do — distorts internal services that respond to internal demand. Treating internal demand as the only relevant driver — as some functional models do — distorts external services that respond to commercial dynamics. The federation acknowledges both kinds of driver and the additional environmental drivers that shape both.

Each unit calibrates its services to the drivers that shape it, rather than to a uniform driver pattern imposed federation-wide. A unit serving external customers responds to commercial dynamics; a unit serving internal consumers responds to internal demand; a unit subject to regulation calibrates its services to regulatory requirements. The federation's portfolio reflects the diversity of driver patterns; the steering rails operate across them.

#### Economic justification

Every service must, over time, justify its cost against the value it produces — the principle of *economic justification*. The basis on which value is measured depends on the basis on which the service exists. Economic basis applies to external services, measured by economic performance against the resources they commit. Demand basis applies to internal services, measured by consumption and value to consuming units. Regulatory basis applies to services that exist because external regulation, internal governance, or organisational principle requires them, measured by the integrity of the function they fulfil.

Without economic justification, the federation accumulates services that no longer produce value commensurate with their cost. With a single basis applied uniformly, services whose existence rests on a different basis are wrongly judged — a regulatory service may have low utilisation because it is succeeding at preventing harm, and judging it by demand metrics produces the wrong answer. Justification on the appropriate basis lets the federation distinguish services that earn their place from services that do not.

The basis on which each service exists is declared explicitly. Measurement follows the basis. The strategic level evaluates services regularly against the appropriate measurement, supported by portfolio data; services that no longer earn their place are candidates for adaptation, repositioning, or retirement.

#### Growth through multiplication and extension

The federation accommodates growth by *multiplying units*, by *deepening the domain map* (adding sub-domains and sub-sub-domains), and by *extending it* (adding new domains as the organisation enters new areas of concern). Where capability the federation needs is not held internally and is not warranted as a new internal service, the federation may also grow by *external sourcing* — contracting the missing capability from outside and managing the relationship through a designated unit. Growth is not accommodated by adding management layers.

This is the structural commitment that distinguishes the FAO from hierarchical organisations under growth pressure. In a hierarchy, growth produces additional management layers as spans of control are exceeded; in the FAO, growth produces additional units, deeper domain maps, wider domain maps, or external relationships. The accountability path remains direct from member to unit lead to strategic level, regardless of how large the federation becomes.

When a unit grows beyond what its lead can hold, the unit splits. When a domain becomes large enough to admit further specialisation, sub-domains separate into their own units. When the organisation enters a new area of concern, the domain map extends to recognise it, and units arise to hold the services the new domain requires. When a capability gap opens that does not warrant a new internal service, the strategic level decides — on the basis of portfolio data, organisational strategy, and the relative cost of internal versus external provision — to source the capability externally; the relationship is then managed through a designated unit. The strategic level stewards these movements through its domain definition function.

### A.2 The Portfolio

The federation maintains a continuous representation of itself — the *portfolio*. The portfolio is the integrated view of everything the organisation comprises: its domains and sub-domains, its units, the services each unit offers, the capabilities each unit holds, the projects currently underway, the assets the organisation owns, the people whose work the organisation depends on, and — where AI participates as accountable entity — the AI participants and the services they provide. The principles below describe what the portfolio is for, how its currency is sustained, and how reporting flows through it.

#### Portfolio as the substrate of coordination

The portfolio is the *substrate on which the federation's coordination operates*. The steering rails act on the portfolio, not on a chart or an inherited org structure. A unit needing a service queries the portfolio; the strategic level evaluating relevance consults the portfolio; finance allocating fixed costs uses the portfolio.

The federation needs to be visible to itself. Without a shared representation, units cannot find services they need, the strategic level cannot steer on accurate data, and finance cannot allocate against reality. The portfolio is what makes the federation legible to its own members and to its own steering.

The portfolio is the source from which dashboards, reports, and analyses are generated. It is not itself a dashboard or a report; it is the structured record. Tools — financial systems, project management systems, strategic dashboards — consume the portfolio as their data source.

#### Portfolio currency

Each unit is responsible for *keeping its portfolio entries current*. Capability declarations, service descriptions, available capacity, and any change in scope or context are maintained as part of the unit's normal operation.

The portfolio's value depends on its currency. A portfolio that lags behind the federation's actual state breaks the coordination logic — units cannot find what they need, the strategic level steers on stale data, finance allocates against a fiction. Currency is not optional administrative overhead; it is what makes the portfolio work.

The unit lead is accountable for the unit's portfolio entries. Updates are made as state changes — when a service is adapted, when capacity changes, when boundaries shift — rather than on a periodic submission cycle. The federation's tooling supports continuous update; the unit's operation incorporates currency as part of routine work.

#### Reporting through the portfolio

*Reporting flows through the portfolio*. Reporting is, where possible, a by-product of work captured by the technology that supports service delivery, rather than a parallel work stream. Where reporting requires human authorship — integrative narrative, strategic analysis, executive briefings — that authorship is itself a service offered by a business management unit, demanded by those who need it.

Reporting that is a parallel work stream consumes capacity that would otherwise produce the work being reported on. Reporting that is a by-product of work consumes only the capacity needed to instrument the work. The federation's design intent is to make reporting structurally informative without inflating it into work that competes with the work itself.

The technology supporting service delivery — project tooling, financial systems, communication platforms — captures the state that the portfolio reflects. Members do not write status reports; the system reflects the state of their work. Where additional analysis or narrative is required, it is commissioned from a unit whose service this is.

### A.3 Accountability and Self-Management

The federation operates by *self-management*. Units determine how they fulfil their mandates without imposed direction from above; direction from the strategic level is the exception, not the rule. Self-management is not a stand-alone principle but the operating consequence of how accountability and responsibility are arranged in the federation. The principles below state that arrangement: how responsibility and accountability are separated and located, how accountability is held at every level of the operational structure, and how self-management coexists with the federation's interactive cooperation through services.

#### Responsibility distributed, accountability concentrated and direct

Self-management requires that *responsibility and accountability be separated and located*. Responsibility — the obligation to perform work to a defined standard — is distributed across the people who do the work. Accountability — the obligation to answer for outcomes — is concentrated at defined points: the unit lead for the unit's mandate, the strategic level for the organisation. The path from responsibility to accountability is direct, with no intermediating layers between the work and the answer it must give.

Conventional hierarchies treat responsibility and accountability as the same thing, escalated upward together, which is what produces the management layers the FAO removes. A federation operating under self-management cannot afford this conflation: where responsibility and accountability are conflated, the latitude to act and the obligation to answer travel together up a chain, and self-management at the unit level becomes impossible. Separating them — distributing responsibility, concentrating accountability — is what makes self-management structurally feasible.

A member is responsible for the quality of their contribution and the integrity of their professional judgement. A unit lead is accountable for the unit's mandate as a whole. The strategic level is accountable for the organisation. The accountability path is structural: a member's work answers to the unit lead, the unit lead's work answers to the strategic level, and there are no layers between.

#### Accountability internalised at every level

Self-management requires that *accountability be internalised at every level* of the operational structure. Every accountable party in the operational structure — the parties at every level whose work the accountability concerns — holds accountability directly for what their work concerns. Responsibility deepens with professional level; accountability follows.

A federation in which accountability is held only by leads — and below that, members merely follow instructions — is not a federation under self-management but a hierarchy with the layers renamed. Self-management requires accountability at every level because self-management *means* every level acting on its own judgement within its own accountability. Internalised accountability is what makes the unit a federation in miniature: members act on their own judgement within their accountability, leads represent the unit, and the structure holds because each level answers for its own work.

As a member's professional level rises — from junior to senior to principal — their responsibility deepens, from their own contribution to guiding others' work to shaping how the unit performs work. A senior member who holds a service or sub-service mandate is content-accountable for that mandate, even though they are not the unit lead. The unit lead remains accountable for the unit as a whole; the senior is accountable for the part they hold within it.

#### Self-management is interactive, not isolated

Self-management is autonomy in *how*, not autonomy in disconnection. Unit autonomy is the autonomy to determine how the unit fulfils its mandate; it is not the autonomy to operate disconnected from the federation. Inter-unit interaction through service consumption is the normal operation of the federation, not an exception to it.

Federation under self-management is sometimes misread as isolation: each unit independent, each unit self-contained, each unit acting on its own without reference to others. This misreading would make the federation an aggregate of small organisations rather than a single organisation. Cooperation through services is what makes the units a federation rather than a collection — and what makes self-management coherent rather than fragmenting.

Units consume each other's services as a matter of course. The service relationship is the structural mechanism through which self-management coexists with federation-wide coherence: consuming units depend on providing units, providing units sustain themselves through consumption, and the federation's coordination emerges from the pattern of these relationships rather than from imposed direction. The strategic level steers through the rails — strategy, governance, reporting, escalation — not through direct intervention in unit work. Where intervention occurs, it is exceptional and visible.

### A.4 The Steering Rails

The federation is steered through four rails that operate together: strategy, governance, reporting, and escalation. None of them is optional; their combination is what allows the federation to act coherently without intermediating management. The functions that intermediating management once performed — directing, regulating, monitoring, resolving — must still be performed in a federation that has removed intermediating management, and the rails distribute these functions across the federation in ways that do not re-introduce the layers. The principles below describe each rail.

#### Strategy as guidance and guardrails

Organisational *strategy* functions both as guidance — the direction toward which units should orient — and as guardrails — the boundary beyond which no unit-level decision is acceptable. Each unit develops its own strategy that extends the organisation's, never conflicts with it.

Strategy at the organisational level alone leaves units without orientation for their own decisions. Strategy at the unit level alone, without an organisational frame, produces a federation whose units pursue divergent ends. Strategy at both levels — with the unit-level strategy extending the organisational — gives the federation direction at every scale while preserving each unit's interpretation within its own scope.

The strategic level forms the organisation's strategy and revises it as the environment changes. Each unit derives its own strategy from the organisation's, calibrated to the unit's mandate. Where a unit's strategy would conflict with the organisation's, the conflict is surfaced and resolved before the unit acts on it.

#### Federation-level governance

Federation-level *governance* is the procedural structure within which the federation as a whole operates. It defines the rules by which units interact, the protocols by which conflicts are surfaced and addressed, and the criteria by which structural decisions are made. It is calibrated to provide structure where structure is needed and to leave space where creativity is needed.

Without federation-level governance, the federation fragments into units operating on incompatible rules. With excessive federation-level governance, the latitude that makes federated expertise valuable is suppressed. Calibration is itself an ongoing concern: governance evolves as the federation evolves, with the strategic level responsible for keeping it appropriate.

Governance is held by the strategic level and applied by every unit. It is not enforced through approval gates; it is operated as a shared protocol that units follow because the federation's coherence depends on it. Where governance is violated, the situation is surfaced through reporting or escalation and addressed.

#### Convened-board mechanism

Structured peer resolution operates through *convened boards*. A board is a temporary assembly of the parties whose work the situation affects, convened to reach agreement on the situation, and dissolved when the situation is resolved. The board has no authority over its members; it is the mechanism through which the parties cooperate toward agreement.

The federation's situations that need resolution are too varied to be served by a single standing committee structure. A standing structure would also be a structural fixture of the kind the FAO is designed to avoid. Convened boards give the federation a flexible mechanism for peer resolution that arises when needed and dissolves when not.

The convened-board mechanism applies to several recurring situations. When a service must adapt — to changing consumer needs, to shifting market conditions, to evolving provider capability — the providing unit and the consuming units convene a board for the duration of the adaptation; the board reaches agreement on what adapts, when, and at what cost to whom, and dissolves when adaptation is complete. Where multiple units operate within a single domain, they may convene a board to surface divergent approaches, overlapping commissions, or differing interpretations of strategy; the alignment is not binding, and its purpose is to make visible to the participating units what they would otherwise miss. The mechanism is general: strategy adaptation among the strategic management units, structural decisions affecting multiple units, and other peer situations are addressed through convened boards. In every case, the board is convened by the parties whose work is affected, has no authority over its members, dissolves when the situation is resolved, and escalates when agreement cannot be reached.

#### Escalation as reserve

Self-management resolves through convened boards. Where boards cannot reach agreement, *escalation* to the strategic level is the reserve. The escalation path is direct, without intermediate routing.

Self-management is the federation's default; escalation is what handles the cases self-management cannot. The directness of the path is what prevents escalation from accumulating into intermediate management layers — each intermediate routing point would become a position, each position a layer. The FAO refuses this accumulation by design.

When a convened board cannot reach agreement, or when a peer situation has no obvious board to convene, the parties escalate to the strategic level. The strategic level resolves the situation directly, without delegating to an intermediate. Frequent escalation indicates a governance gap or strategy gap, and the structural response is to repair the rail at fault rather than to add a layer.

### A.5 The Strategic Level

The strategic level of the FAO is itself composed of units. The CEO unit, the CFO unit, the CTO unit, the COO unit, the CRO unit (resource direction — humans, AI, and tooling), and the CVO unit (accountable for the totality of what the organisation delivers externally) are each strategic management units. A CMO unit is added where marketing direction requires its own unit. The CEO unit holds integrating accountability and sits above the others in residual hierarchical relationship; the other strategic management units are peers at their level. Each strategic management unit operates as a unit: it has a lead, holds defined services, consumes services, declares its mandate in the portfolio. The CRO designation reflects that the federation's resource stewardship extends across humans, AI participants, and tooling rather than being limited to people alone.

#### Strategic management functions

The strategic management units hold *collective accountability for six functions*: strategy formation, boundary enforcement (ensuring unit-level strategies and governance extend the organisation's rather than conflict with them), conflict resolution as the destination for escalation, domain definition and stewardship, relevance control for units and services, and portfolio integration. The CEO unit holds integrating accountability across all six; the other strategic management units hold specialised accountability for the area their service concerns.

The functions are what the federation's strategic level must perform for the federation to operate. They are not allocated rigidly to specific chief officers — different organisations will allocate differently — but they are collectively held at the strategic level. Specifying them gives the federation a checklist of what its strategic level must do, regardless of how the organisation distributes the work.

Strategy formation produces the organisation's strategy and revises it. Boundary enforcement keeps unit strategies aligned. Conflict resolution receives escalations. Domain definition and stewardship maintains the domain map. Relevance control evaluates units and services on the appropriate basis and decides on adaptation, repositioning, or retirement. Portfolio integration maintains the portfolio as the federation's integrated self-representation.

### A.6 The Career Model

#### Two career tracks

The FAO separates *two career tracks* that conventional organisations conflate. The professional track is the default progression and the path most members follow. The management track is separate and gated by management aptitude; it progresses organically with new units rather than from a parallel ladder maintained for compensation purposes.

Conventional organisations route compensation progression through management positions, which produces a management layer populated by people whose actual aptitude is professional rather than managerial. The FAO addresses this by giving the professional track its own progression — depth, recognition, compensation rising with professional level — and reserving the management track for those whose work the organisation actually needs to be management work.

A member progresses through professional levels — junior, senior, principal, and so on, with the specific levels and titles calibrated to the profession. Compensation rises with professional progression; position within the unit reflects professional standing. The professional track does not lead through management; it leads to deeper expertise. The management track is entered by demonstrated management aptitude; new unit lead positions arise when new units are created — when domains grow, when services consolidate or split, when the organisation expands.

#### Responsibility deepens with professional level

As a member's professional level rises, their *responsibility deepens* — for their own work, then for guiding others' work, then for shaping how the unit performs work. Accountability follows: a senior member with a service or sub-service mandate is content-accountable for that mandate.

The career model needs to give professional progression real substance, not just title and compensation. Deepening responsibility is what gives the senior and principal levels their meaning: they hold mandates within the unit, exercise judgement on the unit's behalf, and shape how the unit operates. This is what makes the professional track a genuine alternative to the management track rather than a stalled parallel.

A junior member is responsible for their own contribution. A senior member holds a service or sub-service mandate and is accountable for it. A principal member holds a more complex mandate, possibly spanning several services or shaping the unit's approach to its work. The unit lead remains accountable for the unit as a whole; senior and principal members are accountable for the parts they hold within it.

#### Higher per-position responsibility

Positions in the FAO carry, on average, *more genuine responsibility* than equivalent positions in conventional organisations.

Because accountability is concentrated at unit leads and the strategic level rather than distributed up a management chain, the members within a unit exercise real professional judgement — not judgement subject to routine review and override by intermediating management. The FAO depends on this and rewards it.

The unit lead does not review and approve members' professional decisions; the lead is accountable for the unit's mandate and trusts the members to discharge their own responsibility. Where decisions must be coordinated, the coordination operates through the federation's structures — the unit's governance, the steering rails, convened boards — rather than through routine management approval.

## Part C — FAO and AFS

The FAO stands alone as an organisational argument. An organisation can adopt the FAO at modest scale without any specific technical substrate; the federation of units, the steering rails, the portfolio, and the accountability structure can be operated with the tools any contemporary organisation already uses. The argument of this paper does not depend on AFS for its validity.

At scale, however, the FAO becomes increasingly dependent on a substrate that can carry its steering rails without re-introducing the intermediating layers the model is designed to remove. Governance must be applied across many units; reporting must be aggregated into integrative views; strategy must diffuse from the strategic level to where decisions are actually made; conflicts must surface promptly rather than accumulating below the threshold at which they reach the strategic level. In conventional hierarchies, middle management is the carrier of these functions. The FAO removes middle management; the functions remain.

The Agentic Federation System (AFS) is proposed as the substrate that carries these functions in an FAO operated at scale. AFS describes a federation of AI experts, supervisors, and mergers cooperating under a shared protocol and governance, with accountability and responsibility distributed in a pattern structurally parallel to the FAO. In an FAO supported by an AFS, AI participates in the federation as accountable entity rather than as productivity tool. AI does not only produce content for human members to use; it occupies roles within the steering rails — applying governance, supporting reporting, surfacing conflicts, checking strategic alignment — that would otherwise require human intermediating management.

This is the property that allows the FAO to scale without the federation collapsing back into hierarchy. AI acting as accountable participant can carry coordination load that neither human members (whose work is the substantive output of the federation) nor unit leads (whose attention is the unit's mandate) nor the strategic level (whose attention is the organisation as a whole) can absorb without becoming the very intermediating layer the FAO is designed to remove. The FAO and the AFS are therefore proposed as a co-evolved pair, with FAO providing the organisational model and AFS providing the technical substrate that makes the model viable as the organisation grows.

An organisation may adopt the FAO without the AFS, and an AFS may be deployed within an organisation that does not adopt the FAO. Each is independently meaningful. Their full effect is realised when they are deployed together, because each addresses a dimension of the same underlying shift toward distributed accountability that the other cannot reach.

## Potential Advantages

The FAO is not the first model to propose distributed authority, federated organisation, or the reduction of hierarchy. Its specific contribution lies in the combination of properties below — properties that follow from the model's structural commitments rather than from any single design decision.

- **Reduction of intermediating management without loss of structural clarity.** The FAO removes the layers of hierarchy whose function is information passthrough while retaining the strategic level whose function is strategic accountability and the unit lead role whose function is mandate representation. Structural clarity is preserved where it serves; layering is removed where it does not.

- **Self-management grounded in accountability internalised at every level.** Members, senior members holding service mandates, unit leads, and the strategic level each hold accountability directly for what their work concerns. Self-management is not a separate principle imposed on top of the structure; it is the operating consequence of how accountability is arranged.

- **Bundling rather than duplication of capability.** The single-service rule consolidates capability that would otherwise scatter across multiple units, preserving functional depth and avoiding the silent costs of parallel work performed in different units to nominally similar ends.

- **Internal market as a first-class structural element.** The FAO recognises the internal market as structurally distinct from the external market, with its own driver and its own dynamic. Internal services are subject to demand discipline; external services are subject to commercial discipline; the federation operates both simultaneously without reducing one to the other.

- **Organic management progression.** New unit lead positions arise when new units do, rather than from a parallel ladder maintained for compensation purposes. Management progression is tied to the organisation's actual structural needs, and the professional track provides the progression path for the people whose career is depth in their discipline.

- **Higher per-position responsibility.** Because accountability is concentrated rather than distributed up a management chain, the members within a unit exercise real professional judgement — not judgement subject to routine review and override by intermediating management. The FAO depends on this and rewards it.

- **Portfolio as continuous self-representation.** The federation maintains its own structural state visibly and currently, available to any unit whose work depends on it. Reporting becomes a by-product of work rather than a parallel work stream, supporting continuous coordination without competing with the work itself.

- **Compatibility with AI as accountable participant.** When operated with the AFS as technical substrate, the FAO accommodates AI not as productivity tooling but as accountable entity within the federation. This is the property that allows the model to scale without re-emerging middle management as the carrier of coordination load.

- **Faster adaptation through living structure.** Domains, units, and services are maintained as a living portfolio rather than fossilised in an inherited org chart. The organisation's structural composition changes as its environment changes, on the basis of explicit decisions by the strategic level against current data, rather than through accumulated drift.

## Challenges to Address

The challenges below are the most immediately visible open questions of the FAO. They are not a complete risk assessment; they are the points at which the model's claims will need to be tested most carefully when an organisation considers adoption.

- **Calibration of the domain map.** The domain map is the first structuring layer of the federation, and the quality of the map shapes everything downstream — which units exist, which services they provide, where boundaries fall. Whether the strategic level can construct and maintain a domain map that reflects the organisation's actual structure of concern, and that evolves coherently as the organisation grows, is open. A poorly calibrated domain map produces units whose mandates are unclear, services whose boundaries are contested, and a portfolio whose structure obscures rather than reveals.

- **Measurement of unit and service value.** Some services have clean economic measurement; some have demand-based measurement; some have regulatory measurement. The FAO requires each service to be measured on the basis appropriate to it, and the federation's relevance control depends on these measurements being made well. How to measure internal value, in particular, in a way that supports retire-or-retain decisions without distorting the work the service performs, is among the most consequential open questions.

- **Governance calibration.** Governance must contain enough to prevent fragmentation and not so much as to constrain the creativity that makes federated expertise valuable. The correct calibration depends on the organisation's composition, the maturity of its units, the nature of its work, and the rate at which its environment changes. The federation must have mechanisms to detect when governance is failing in either direction; the design of such mechanisms is open.

- **Convened-board calibration.** The convened-board mechanism is general — it applies to service adaptation, alignment within domains, strategy adaptation, and other peer situations. The federation must have governance that determines when a board is convened, how it is composed, what it can settle, and when it dissolves. Too readily convened, boards become a shadow committee structure; too rarely convened, situations that need resolution drift unaddressed. Holding alignment-style boards distinct from steering and from binding decision-making is itself part of the calibration.

- **Portfolio currency.** The portfolio's value depends on its currency, and currency depends on every unit maintaining its own entries. Where currency lapses, the federation's coordination logic begins to fail. The mechanisms by which currency is sustained — governance, tooling, accountability for currency itself — are open in their detail and likely to be among the most consequential implementation choices an adopting organisation makes.

- **Career model implementation.** The FAO's career model presupposes that compensation and recognition can be tied to professional progression as substantively as conventional models tie them to management progression. Whether this is achievable in organisations operating in markets where compensation conventions are set externally — by industry norms, by competitor practice, by labour-market expectations — is open. The model is structurally clear; its implementation in compensation practice requires further development.

- **Multi-organisation structures.** Where multiple organisations operate together — a corporate group, a parent and its subsidiaries, federated entities under a shared governance — each organisation is its own FAO, and the relationships between them are inter-organisational service relationships supported by external governance such as board representation and shareholding. A parent FAO may shape a subsidiary FAO's strategy and governance through these mechanisms. The detail of how active parent shaping coexists with subsidiary self-management, and where the limits of each lie, depends on the specifics of the corporate relationship and is an open question for adopting organisations.

- **Transition from existing hierarchical organisations.** Most organisations adopting the FAO will not be founded under it; they will be transitioning from hierarchical structures. The transition path — how the existing organisation's activity is mapped to a domain map, how duplicated services are consolidated, how middle management roles are evaluated as passthrough versus genuine management, how the unit-lead layer is established, how governance and frame conditions are formalised — is a substantial undertaking and is not addressed in this paper. It is among the open questions an adopting organisation faces and likely warrants treatment in a paper of its own.

- **FAO viability without AFS at large scale.** The FAO is internally consistent at any scale, but its viability at large scale depends on a substrate that can carry the steering rails without re-emergence of middle management. Whether a large FAO can operate without the AFS or an equivalent substrate, and what the threshold of scale is at which the substrate becomes necessary, is open. Organisations adopting the FAO at scale should consider this question explicitly rather than discover its consequences in operation.
